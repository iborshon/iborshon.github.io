The bridge between physics and data

My work sits at one recurring boundary: where a hard scientific measurement is too noisy, too sparse, or too expensive to read directly, and a model has to recover the structure underneath. I treat machine learning not as a black box but as a physics-constrained instrument, where simulation supplies the ground truth that experimental data alone cannot.

At the University of Arizona I develop Vision Transformers, U-Net, and Mask R-CNN pipelines for atomic-column segmentation, defect detection, and interface classification in STEM/TEM micrographs of electrochemical materials, linking what the image shows to degradation, state-of-health, and cycle life in lithium batteries.

Before that I spent years on the thermal-energy side: 3D CFD of solar cavity receivers, ground-source heat pump software used by engineers nationally, and techno-economic modeling with ORNL. That breadth is the point. The same instinct, let the physics constrain the model, carries across all of it.

PhD Dissertation

Physics-Informed Deep Learning for Atomic-Scale Characterization of Electrochemical Energy Materials

This dissertation develops a family of computer-vision pipelines that bridge high-resolution electron microscopy and physics-based simulation to characterize electrochemical materials at atomic resolution. The core challenge: experimental STEM/TEM images are too noisy and too sparsely labeled for standard supervised learning. The solution is a simulation-to-experiment transfer framework in which multislice STEM simulations (PyQSTEM) and DFT calculations generate physically consistent ground-truth annotations. These synthetic datasets train Vision Transformer, U-Net, and Mask R-CNN architectures that generalize to real experimental data. Applications span solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) microstructure in lithium-ion batteries, elemental composition prediction in high-entropy oxides, and grain-boundary evolution in multi-principal-element alloys — each linking atomic-scale imaging features to macroscopic properties such as degradation and cycle life.
